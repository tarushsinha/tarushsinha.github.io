EBITDA stands for **Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization**. It's a metric used to evaluate a company's operating performance by looking at how much cash it generates from its core operations, before considering certain financial factors.

EBITDA excludes the following:

- Interest

- Taxes

- Deprecation

- Amortization

As a result EBITDA gives a clearer picture of a company’s operational efficiency and cash flow generation, independent of its financing choices, tax situation, and accounting methods for asset values.

### **Limitations**

- It doesn't tell the whole story of a company's financial health. You still need to consider other factors like net income, debt levels, and growth prospects.

- It can be manipulated by aggressive accounting practices. Investors should always be skeptical of overly high EBITDA figures.

- While useful for comparing companies within the same industry, it can be misleading when comparing companies in different industries with different capital expenditures.

### EBIT

EBIT stands for **Earnings Before Interest and Taxes, **and includes the following:

- Revenue

- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

- Operating Expenses

---

### EBT

EBT stands for **Earnings Before Taxes, **and includes the following:

- Revenue

- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

- Operating Expenses

- Depreciation

- Amortization

### **EBITDA Margin**

The EBITDA margin takes the concept of EBITDA one step further by expressing it as a **percentage of a company's revenue**. It tells you **how much of every dollar earned through sales translates to cash flow before considering interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization**.

EBITDA Margin = EBITDA / Revenue * 100%

A higher EBITDA margin generally indicates **stronger operational efficiency and cash flow generation** because it means the company is converting a larger portion of its revenue into immediate cash. This can be attractive to investors, as it suggests the company can reinvest funds in growth or easily cover its financial obligations.

**Additional Notes:**

- Analyzing the trend of a company's EBITDA margin over time can be more insightful than relying on a single figure. A consistent or increasing margin indicates positive signs, while a declining margin could raise concerns about operational efficiency or future cash flow.

- While a high margin is generally positive, the "good" range for the EBITDA margin can vary significantly depending on the industry. Technology companies, for example, often have higher margins than manufacturing companies due to their lower reliance on physical assets.

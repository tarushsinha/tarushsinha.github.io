Capitated Systems introduce a payment model where healthcare providers receive a **fixed, pre-determined amount of money per patient**, regardless of the volume of services provided. The goal is to **shift the focus from quantity to quality** and encourage preventative care and efficient resource management.

**How it works:**

- Health plans (like HMOs or ACOs) contract with providers (doctors, clinics) at a pre-negotiated rate per member enrolled. This "capitation payment" covers a defined set of services for a specific period, often annually.

- Providers are responsible for managing the health of their assigned patients within the budget they receive. This includes delivering preventive care, managing chronic conditions, and coordinating referrals to specialists when needed.

- Providers have an incentive to keep their patients healthy and avoid unnecessary services because they save money when their patients stay well.

P**otential benefits:**

- Reduced healthcare costs: By promoting preventative care and managing resources efficiently, capitation can lead to lower overall healthcare spending.

- Improved quality of care: Providers have a vested interest in keeping patients healthy and avoiding complications, which can lead to better overall health outcomes.

- Increased focus on primary care: Capitation promotes a strong primary care foundation, as primary care physicians play a key role in managing patients' health and coordinating care.

**Risks:**

- Financial risk for providers: If patients require more services than expected, providers may lose money under the capitation payment.

- Potential for underuse of services: Providers may be reluctant to refer patients to specialists or order expensive tests to avoid exceeding their budget.

- Administrative complexity: Capitation systems require complex contracts, data analysis, and care coordination, which can add administrative burden.
